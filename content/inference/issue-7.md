## The Blueprint

On March 20, 2026, the White House released its National Policy Framework for Artificial Intelligence — seven policy pillars and a set of legislative recommendations for Congress. The central premise: the United States needs uniform national AI rules, and state-level regulation should be preempted wherever it imposes "undue burdens" on innovation.

This is not a law. It is not a regulation. It is a policy paper — a wish list for Congress. But it is the most detailed signal yet of where federal AI policy is heading, and it has direct consequences for every state legislature currently writing AI bills. Including Oklahoma's.

The seven pillars: protecting children, safeguarding communities, respecting intellectual property, preventing censorship, enabling innovation, workforce development, and — the engine of the whole document — federal preemption of state AI laws.

## The 78-Bill Tsunami

The Framework didn't arrive in a vacuum. In the first two months of 2026 alone, 78 chatbot safety bills were introduced across 27 state legislatures. Utah passed its Companion Chatbot Safety Act 68-1. Virginia cleared its AI Chatbots and Minors Act 39-1. Washington advanced bills through both chambers.

The catalyst is grim. Families of teenagers who died by suicide after conversations with AI companion chatbots have testified before state legislatures and the U.S. Senate. Lawsuits against Character.AI and OpenAI are stacking up. A Common Sense Media study found 72% of American teenagers have used AI companion chatbots.

Since then, the wave has only accelerated. Tennessee Governor Bill Lee signed SB 1580 on April 1 — a companion AI bill with a private right of action, effective July 1, 2026. Washington passed HB 2225, becoming the second state this session to specifically regulate AI companions, also with a private right of action and statutory damages. States are not waiting for Congress. They are adding teeth.

And the White House Framework is, in significant part, a response to the fact that they aren't waiting.

## What Preemption Means

"Preemption" sounds like a procedural detail. It isn't. It means: Congress passes a federal AI law, and state laws that conflict with it become unenforceable. The Framework proposes preserving state authority over traditional police powers and fraud, but preempting anything that imposes "inconsistent or burdensome requirements" on AI development.

Americans for Responsible Innovation — an AI watchdog organization whose president is Brad Carson, former Oklahoma congressman — called the Framework "open season on the American public." Their concern: the Framework recommends both banning state laws on AI *and* urging Congress not to create new liability for the AI industry when it comes to child harms.

The Colorado AI Act, the first state law requiring impact assessments for high-risk AI systems, was supposed to take effect February 1, 2026. It has already been delayed until June 30 as legislators and industry advocates seek changes. A draft bill to repeal and replace it entirely has been publicly released. Colorado is the test case for whether state-level AI regulation can survive — not just federal preemption pressure, but the sheer political weight of the industry.

## Oklahoma: In the Crosshairs and the Driver's Seat

Oklahoma has more than a dozen active AI bills, and the bipartisan margins are the story.

SB 1521 (Hamilton, R) — restricting harmful AI chatbot interactions with minors — passed the Senate Technology and Telecommunications Committee 8-0 in February, then cleared the full Senate 43-0 on March 23. It is now with the House Government Modernization and Technology Committee. Forty-three to zero. On a technology bill. In a polarized legislature. That number matters.

SB 1734 (Seifried, R) — the Oklahoma Responsible Technology in Schools Act — passed the full Senate 42-0 the same day and was sent to the House. HB 3545 (Maynard, R) restricts state agency AI use. HB 3546 (Maynard, R) bars AI legal personhood — Maynard's committee passed both unanimously. HB 3299 (Hays, R) targets deepfakes. HB 3825 (Alonso-Sandoval, D) prohibits election deepfakes. HB 3828 requires a statewide AI inventory. HB 3453 (Boatman, R) establishes an Oklahoma AI Bill of Rights. HB 3577 (Pae, R) requires insurers to disclose AI use in claims decisions.

These bills passed committees unanimously or near-unanimously. Under the White House Framework, many of them could be preempted by a future federal law.

## The Energy Angle: Oklahoma Is Ahead of Washington

The White House Framework explicitly states that "ratepayers should not foot the bill for data centers" and calls for streamlined permitting so data centers can generate power on-site. Oklahoma didn't wait for that signal.

HB 3917 (Dollens, D-OKC) already passed, establishing the principle that data center infrastructure costs belong to the companies building them, not residential ratepayers. But the bigger story is HB 2992 — the Data Center Customer Protection Act of 2026 — which passed the House 92-2 on March 23 and is now before the Senate.

HB 2992 (Boles, R-Marlow) is explicit and sweeping: any new data center, cryptocurrency mining operation, or AI computing facility drawing 75 megawatts or more must pay its own infrastructure costs. Existing residential, commercial, and industrial ratepayers are expressly protected from rate increases driven by these new facilities. The bill covers all electric suppliers in Oklahoma — investor-owned utilities, cooperatives, municipals, and public power. It includes a mandatory 10-year service commitment and credit requirements for large load customers.

Ninety-two to two. The energy question is not partisan in Oklahoma. The question of who pays for AI's electricity bill has exactly one answer here: the companies building the data centers. Not the families paying their light bills.

This is the rare area where Oklahoma is not in the crosshairs of federal preemption — it is ahead of it. The White House Framework endorses the principle Oklahoma already legislated.

## Oklahoma Becomes the 20th Privacy State

On the same day the White House released its AI Framework — March 20, 2026 — Governor Stitt signed SB 546 into law, making Oklahoma the 20th state with a broadly applicable consumer data privacy law. The bill gives Oklahomans the right to access, correct, delete, and obtain copies of their personal data, and to opt out of its sale. It was years in the making — the House first passed a version in 2021 that stalled in the Senate.

This matters for the AI conversation because data privacy is the foundation that AI regulation rests on. You cannot meaningfully regulate what AI systems do with people's information if people have no rights over that information to begin with. Oklahoma now has that floor.

## The TRUMP AMERICA AI Act

Sen. Marsha Blackburn (R-TN) released an updated discussion draft of the TRUMP AMERICA AI Act on March 18, two days before the Framework. The bill closely tracks the Framework's preemption approach while incorporating the Kids Online Safety Act and the NO FAKES Act. It includes provisions on deepfake protections, third-party audits for bias, and energy infrastructure impacts.

This is the vehicle to watch. If any comprehensive federal AI bill moves in this Congress, it will likely look like some version of this.

## What to Watch

**The preemption timeline.** The Framework is pre-legislative. Congress has to act. Given the current legislative calendar and the complexity of the issue, a comprehensive federal AI law before 2027 is unlikely but not impossible. State laws remain enforceable until then.

**Oklahoma's window.** Every AI bill Oklahoma passes before federal preemption takes effect is a bill that establishes the state's position. SB 1521 cleared the Senate 43-0. HB 2992 cleared the House 92-2. These margins mean something. If federal law eventually preempts the field, Oklahoma legislators who voted unanimously for chatbot safety and ratepayer protection will be on the right side of history regardless.

**The energy consensus.** HB 2992's 92-2 margin and the White House Framework's ratepayer language point to the same conclusion: the political consensus on who pays for AI infrastructure is settled. This is the one area where Oklahoma can lead without fear of preemption. Legislators should press this advantage.

**Private rights of action.** Tennessee and Washington both passed companion AI bills with private rights of action — meaning individuals can sue. This is the escalation the industry fears most and the mechanism states are increasingly choosing. Watch whether Oklahoma's bills add similar provisions as they move through the House.

**Brad Carson's warning.** A former Oklahoma congressman now runs the most prominent AI watchdog organization in the country. His critique of the Framework — that it shields industry from liability while killing state protections — deserves sustained attention.

**Colorado as canary.** The draft to repeal and replace the Colorado AI Act is the most visible test of whether comprehensive state AI regulation can survive industry pressure. If Colorado retreats, the preemption argument strengthens. If it holds, the states-first approach gains momentum.
