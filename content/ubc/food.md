Forty million Americans experience food insecurity. Not in some failed state — in the wealthiest nation in human history. Meanwhile, we throw away 30-40% of our food supply. This isn't a production problem. It's a distribution problem dressed up as inevitability.

UBC's food component guarantees access to nutritious, sustainable food for all citizens. Not as welfare. Not as charity with means-testing designed to humiliate. As infrastructure — the same way we treat roads and water lines. You don't apply for permission to drive on a highway. You shouldn't have to prove your poverty to eat.

## Why This Is Foundational

Hunger makes everything else impossible. You can't learn on an empty stomach. You can't think clearly when you're worried about feeding your children. Food insecurity is a cognitive tax that falls hardest on people who can least afford to pay it, and it cascades into every other system: health, education, productivity, mental health, family stability.

When we talk about preparing society for the AI transition, we're talking about a population that needs to be adaptive, creative, and engaged. Malnourished people aren't any of those things. This isn't sentiment — it's structural.

## The AI Opportunity

AI and automation are transforming agriculture: precision farming, vertical agriculture, supply chain optimization, waste reduction through better demand prediction. These technologies could dramatically reduce the cost of feeding everyone well. The question, as always, is who benefits.

If AI-driven agriculture concentrates further into corporate monocultures, it makes the system more fragile and the access problem worse. If it's deployed to strengthen local and regional food systems — community farms, cooperative distribution, school nutrition programs — it becomes genuine infrastructure for human flourishing.

## What We're Not Saying

We're not prescribing what people eat. We're not imagining government-issued meal plans. We're saying that in a society wealthy enough to feed everyone, the choice not to is a policy failure. The mechanism — whether it's expanded SNAP without stigma, universal school meals, public market infrastructure, or something we haven't thought of yet — is what this conversation is for.
