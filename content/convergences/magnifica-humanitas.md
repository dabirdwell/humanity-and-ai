## The Tradition

On May 25, 2026, Pope Leo XIV released *Magnifica Humanitas* ("Magnificent Humanity"), his first encyclical — a 40,000-word document representing the most authoritative form of papal teaching. It was presented at the Vatican with Christopher Olah, co-founder of Anthropic's interpretability team, in attendance. The encyclical is described as the fruit of 10 years of dialogue on ethics between the Vatican and the technology industry.

## The Convergent Conclusion

Leo calls for AI to be "disarmed" — a word he chose deliberately because "this moment needs words capable of attracting attention, awakening consciences, and indicating paths forward for humanity." AI must be "freed from logics that turn it into an instrument of domination, exclusion, and death."

The encyclical's core claim aligns with Foundation's: human beings must come before technology. The future of humanity cannot be left in the hands of a few wealthy technology leaders. New forms of exploitation hide behind AI infrastructure — from hidden labor to rare mineral mining to data taken without genuine consent.

Leo writes that human beings "always transcend the sum of their achievements, their data profiles and any possible technical simulation, because they are called to a fullness of life that finds its truth in relationship and its fulfillment in a constant and sincere gift of oneself."

Foundation's Generative Freedom makes the same structural claim in secular language: mattering cannot be reduced to market utility, and a society that treats persons as data profiles to be optimized has inverted the constitutional relationship between people and systems.

Quoting Tolkien's Gandalf, Leo describes the responsibility of those living in consequential times: "uprooting the evil in the fields that we know, so that those who live after may have clean earth to till." Foundation's Three layers of freedom are an attempt to describe what clean earth looks like — and what it takes to till it.

## Where It Differs

The encyclical grounds human dignity in theological claims Foundation does not make. Leo's argument ultimately rests on the conviction that persons are "called to a fullness of life" — a claim that presupposes a caller. Foundation makes no such claim. Its argument for human dignity is structural and empirical: societies that fail to guarantee meaning collapse, regardless of theological commitments.

Catholic social teaching addresses the universal church — 1.4 billion people across every nation. Foundation addresses the civic structures of one state as a proof of concept. The scale of ambition is different, and so is the theory of change.

The encyclical calls for international regulation. Foundation calls for local implementation first, with regulation emerging from practice rather than preceding it. These are different sequencing strategies for similar goals.
