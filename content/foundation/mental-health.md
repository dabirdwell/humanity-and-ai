We treat symptoms because we can't afford to treat causes. That's not a slogan — it's the operating logic of mental healthcare in America, and it's failing on a scale that should terrify anyone paying attention.

*Marcus is not one person.* He's a 34-year-old Army veteran back in the small town he grew up in. Three tours, two deployments to active combat zones. He knows something is wrong. He's tried twice to get into the VA's mental health program — the waitlist is months long. His town doesn't have a single therapist who takes his insurance. He started drinking to sleep. His marriage is fracturing. His wife doesn't know if she's watching her husband drown or just watching him make choices. Neither does he.

Marcus lives in rural Oklahoma, where the nearest psychiatrist might be two hours away. You'd need a car that runs, a day off work you can't afford to lose, and insurance that will actually cover the visit — a three-way alignment that most people in a mental health crisis cannot manage. So they don't go. They wait until it becomes a 911 call, an ER visit, a jail intake.

Foundation is built on the premise that there is a minimum set of conditions people need to thrive. Mental health support is one of [sixteen components](/foundation/) in this framework — broken out separately from healthcare because America systematically treats it as secondary, and that treatment is itself a design choice we can change.

## The Numbers Are Staggering

122 million Americans live in areas formally designated as mental health professional shortage areas. Not areas with long waits — areas with a federally certified shortage. Only 26% of mental health care needs in this country are being met. The other 74% go unaddressed: festering in homes, spilling into emergency rooms, ending in overdoses, incarceration, and quiet losses of lives that never had to go that way.

Insurance parity laws exist on paper and fail in practice. Therapy is out of reach for most working people. Addiction treatment is underfunded, stigmatized, and too often criminalized rather than medicalized. Every dollar spent on criminalizing addiction rather than treating it is a dollar wasted and a life damaged.

## What's Coming Will Be Worse

So that's where we already are. Now add what's coming: mass economic disruption from the AI transition. Accelerating uncertainty. The disorientation of watching machines do what you trained years to do. A loneliness epidemic amplified by algorithmic social media. The mental health demands of the next decade will dwarf anything we've seen.

The opioid crisis was a preview. When entire industries and communities collapsed in deindustrialized America, the mental health infrastructure wasn't there to absorb the shock. People found other ways to manage unbearable pain. Without investment now — proactive, serious, structural investment — the AI transition will produce its own wave. And we will call it, again, a crisis of character rather than a failure of systems.

## What We're Building

Foundation's mental health component treats comprehensive psychological support as a citizenship right — accessible, destigmatized, evidence-based, and integrated with broader healthcare infrastructure. Addiction treatment is part of this, not separate from it, because addiction is a public health issue, not a criminal justice issue.

AI can extend the reach of mental health services — chatbots for triage, therapeutic apps for ongoing support, pattern detection for early intervention. But AI cannot replace human connection in healing. The model is augmentation: AI handles scale and access while human practitioners provide the relational depth that actually heals.

The key insight is upstream investment. It costs less to provide comprehensive mental health support than it costs to manage the crisis that results from not providing it. Emergency rooms, jails, disability claims, lost productivity, shattered families — we're already paying for the absence of this infrastructure. We're just paying in the worst possible way.

## What We Need From You

Therapists, addiction counselors, people in recovery, public health researchers, and especially people who've tried to access mental health care and been failed by the system — your experience is the data we need. What's broken? What works? What would you build if you could start from scratch?

This is citizen-developed work. Here are directions we're exploring — tell us what's missing:

- **AI-assisted triage and crisis support** — not replacing therapists, but extending their reach. Chatbots that can identify crisis signals and connect people to humans immediately, available 24/7, in every zip code
- **Embedded mental health in primary care** — instead of the three-way alignment of car, day off, and insurance, what if mental health screening and support were part of every doctor visit, every school, every workplace?
- **Decriminalize addiction entirely** — redirect enforcement budgets to evidence-based treatment. Portugal did this in 2001 and saw drug-related deaths drop by over 80%. The data is in.
