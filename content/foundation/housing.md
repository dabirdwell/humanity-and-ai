Housing in America has become an extraction machine. In most cities, rent or mortgage consumes 30-50% of household income. Housing "investment" has been reframed as the primary wealth-building vehicle for the middle class, which means housing prices must perpetually rise, which means housing must perpetually become less affordable. The incentive structure is mathematically hostile to the people who need housing most.

UBC's housing component starts from a different premise: shelter is infrastructure, not a speculative asset. Every citizen has the right to safe, affordable housing. Not luxury housing. Not housing that requires 40% of your income to maintain. Housing that provides stability — the platform from which everything else in life becomes possible.

## Why Housing Is the Multiplier

Stable housing is the single strongest predictor of outcomes across nearly every other domain. Children in stable housing perform better in school. Adults with stable housing are healthier, more employable, and less likely to experience mental health crises. Stable housing reduces emergency room visits, incarceration rates, and social service costs.

The economic research on "Housing First" programs — giving people housing unconditionally and then addressing other issues — consistently shows that it's cheaper than the emergency systems we use instead. We pay more to manage homelessness than it would cost to prevent it. This isn't a values argument. It's an accounting argument.

## The AI Transition Housing Problem

Mass economic disruption will create housing instability at scale. When industries contract, local economies that depend on them contract too. Property values crash in some areas. Rent spikes in others as displaced workers migrate to opportunity centers. Without housing infrastructure designed to absorb economic shocks, the AI transition will produce a housing crisis on top of everything else.

## Models That Work

Community land trusts. Public housing that's actually maintained and destigmatized. Cooperative housing models. Mixed-income developments that don't segregate by economic status. Vienna's social housing system, which houses 60% of its population in publicly subsidized housing that's desirable, well-maintained, and economically sustainable. These models exist. They're proven. They're not implemented at scale because the political economy of housing speculation is powerful.
