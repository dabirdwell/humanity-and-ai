A society that can build skyscrapers but can't house its people has a priorities problem, not a resources problem.

*Rosa is not one person.* She's a home health aide — she cares for elderly people in their homes so they can stay out of nursing facilities. The work is essential. It pays $11.50 an hour. The cheapest one-bedroom near her clients rents for $900 a month. She works five days a week and lives with her sister's family, two adults and three kids in a two-bedroom apartment, because there is no other option. She's been on the affordable housing waiting list for three years. The list has 2,000 names on it.

In Oklahoma — not California, not New York — a minimum wage worker would need to put in 93 hours a week just to afford a one-bedroom apartment. That's more than two full-time jobs, for a single room. In 2024, nearly one in four people counted as homeless in Tulsa were employed at the time of their count. They had jobs. They just couldn't afford a roof.

This is not a housing shortage. It is a policy failure. Wages haven't moved in 16 years in Oklahoma. Rent has risen 60% in that same period. The math was always going to produce this outcome.

Foundation is built on the premise that there is a minimum set of conditions people need to thrive. Housing is one of [sixteen components](/foundation/) in this framework, and the argument is simple: shelter is infrastructure, not a speculative asset. Every citizen has the right to safe, affordable housing — the platform from which everything else in life becomes possible.

## Why Housing Is the Multiplier

Rosa cannot build a stable life, save money, maintain her health, or show up reliably for the people who depend on her if she can't first establish where she sleeps. Housing is not a reward for economic success. It is the foundation that makes economic participation possible.

And the data confirms what common sense already tells you: stable housing is the single strongest predictor of outcomes across nearly every other domain. Children in stable housing perform better in school. Adults with stable housing are healthier, more employable, and less likely to experience mental health crises. Stable housing reduces emergency room visits, incarceration rates, and social service costs.

The economic research on "Housing First" programs — giving people housing unconditionally and then addressing other issues — consistently shows that it's cheaper than the emergency systems we use instead. We pay more to manage homelessness than it would cost to prevent it. This isn't just a values argument. It's an accounting argument.

## What's Coming

Mass economic disruption from the AI transition will create housing instability at scale. When industries contract, local economies that depend on them contract too. Property values crash in some areas. Rent spikes in others as displaced workers migrate to opportunity centers. Without housing infrastructure designed to absorb economic shocks, the AI transition will produce a housing crisis on top of everything else.

## Models That Already Work

This isn't a problem without solutions — it's a problem without political will to implement them. Community land trusts. Public housing that's actually maintained and destigmatized. Cooperative housing models. Mixed-income developments that don't segregate by economic status. Vienna's social housing system, which houses 60% of its population in publicly subsidized housing that's desirable, well-maintained, and economically sustainable. These models exist. They're proven.

They're not implemented at scale because the political economy of housing speculation is powerful. Housing "investment" has been reframed as the primary wealth-building vehicle for the middle class, which means housing prices must perpetually rise, which means housing must perpetually become less affordable. The incentive structure is mathematically hostile to the people who need housing most.

## What We Need From You

Housing intersects with everything: land use policy, construction labor, environmental sustainability, community design, disability access. What does "affordable" actually mean in your community? What models preserve community while providing stability? What do people who've experienced housing insecurity know that policymakers don't?

This is citizen-developed work. Here are some of the models we think deserve attention:

- **Community land trusts** — nonprofit ownership of land with affordable housing on it, keeping prices stable permanently instead of letting speculation drive them up. Over 280 CLTs already operate in the US.
- **Vienna's social housing model** — 60% of Vienna's population lives in publicly subsidized housing that's desirable, well-maintained, and mixed-income. It works at scale. What would an American adaptation look like?
- **AI-optimized modular construction** — prefab housing designed by AI for local conditions, cutting construction costs and time while maintaining quality. Some companies are already building homes in days rather than months.
