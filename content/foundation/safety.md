You can't learn if you're afraid. You can't create if you're surviving. You can't participate in democracy if your daily reality is organized around avoiding harm. Safety — real safety, not the performative kind that justifies surveillance — is the bedrock of every other component in this framework.

UBC's safety component isn't about more police or bigger prisons. It's about comprehensive public safety that addresses root causes: community-based support systems, violence intervention programs, conflict resolution infrastructure, and the kind of deep social investment that makes most crime economically irrational.

## Why This Changes with AI

The AI transition introduces safety challenges we haven't seen before. Deepfakes that can destroy reputations overnight. Autonomous systems making decisions about who gets flagged, detained, or denied services. Algorithmic bias embedded in predictive policing that perpetuates the exact inequities it claims to solve.

At the same time, AI offers safety possibilities we've never had. Real-time environmental monitoring. Early warning systems for everything from domestic violence patterns to infrastructure failure. Translation and communication tools that reduce the friction that leads to conflict.

The question isn't whether AI affects safety. It's whether we design safety systems that use AI to serve communities or to control them.

## The Cybernetic Lens

Safety, in Stafford Beer's Viable System Model, is a System 1 function — the operational base that everything else depends on. If the base is unstable, no amount of coordination or strategic planning at higher levels matters. You have to get this right first.

But safety also has a System 3 function: it needs feedback loops. Communities must be able to signal when safety measures are failing or when they've become the threat. A safety system that can't hear "this isn't working" isn't a safety system — it's an occupation.

## What This Looks Like

Comprehensive public safety means physical security from violence, but also emotional security from institutional abuse. It means children safe in schools, workers safe on job sites, people safe in their homes. It means protection from predatory financial systems, from environmental hazards, from the thousand small violences of a society that treats people as expendable.

It means safety *from* the systems that are supposed to provide safety, when those systems malfunction. Accountability isn't optional. It's structural.

## What We're Missing

We have a framework here, not a policy manual. What does community-based safety actually look like in rural Oklahoma versus urban Detroit versus tribal nations? What models are already working that we should be learning from? What are we getting wrong?
