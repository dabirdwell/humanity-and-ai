In most of America, if you can't drive, you can't participate. You can't get to work, to school, to the doctor, to the grocery store. Car ownership is a de facto requirement for citizenship, and it's one of the most regressive costs in the economy — car payments, insurance, fuel, maintenance falling heaviest on those who can least afford it.

UBC's transportation component treats mobility as public infrastructure. Not a consumer product. Not a privilege of geography. Infrastructure — like roads themselves, available to everyone.

## What This Looks Like

> "I missed dialysis twice last month. The van didn't come. There's no other way. My neighbor drove me the third time but she works days now and can't always do it."
> — Rural Oklahoma patient, Oklahoma Health Care Authority testimony on Medicaid transportation, 2024

Harold is 71, lives outside Ardmore, and has end-stage kidney disease. He gets dialysis three times a week. The medical transport service is contracted, chronically understaffed, and frequently late or absent. When he misses a session, he spends the next two days in a level of physical distress his grandchildren try not to look at directly.

His granddaughter, Tasha, turned down a job in town — the best offer she'd had in two years — because she'd have to leave before 7am and Harold can't drive himself anymore. She's 26. The job paid $18 an hour. There's no bus route between their house and town. There is no bus route.

Half of America is Harold's zip code: no transit, no options, no workaround that doesn't cost someone else something they can't spare. Mobility isn't a preference. It's the infrastructure that either connects you to opportunity and care, or quietly forecloses it while nobody calls it a policy choice.

## The Autonomous Vehicle Opportunity

Self-driving electric vehicles are coming. The technology is converging toward viability, and when it arrives at scale, it represents the most significant transportation transformation since the automobile replaced the horse. Fleets of autonomous electric vehicles could provide on-demand mobility to every citizen at a fraction of the current cost of car ownership.

The question is ownership. If autonomous fleets are owned by three corporations that extract maximum revenue, we've just replaced one dependency with another. If they're publicly owned or cooperatively structured — transportation as a utility, like electricity — then autonomous vehicles become genuine liberation infrastructure. Especially in rural areas and small towns where public transit has never been economically viable in its traditional form.

## Rural Reality

This isn't a coastal urban fantasy. Rural and small-town America has been systematically excluded from transportation investment. Oklahoma has no passenger rail, minimal bus service outside the metros, and vast distances between essential services. For elderly, disabled, or economically constrained residents, isolation isn't a metaphor — it's Tuesday.

Universal transportation means solving the rural problem, not just adding more subway lines in cities that already have them. Autonomous vehicles and on-demand ride services may actually solve this better than traditional transit ever could — if they're designed as public infrastructure rather than premium products.

## Energy Integration

Transportation and energy are inseparable. Electric vehicle fleets need charging infrastructure powered by sustainable energy. This connects directly to Component 7 (Sustainable Energy) and to Phoenix Wells — where geothermal energy could power the charging networks that power the vehicles that connect communities.
